Part 1

Pete's Wager

Imagine you are at the pub with your friend Pete. He suggests a bet: he will toss a coin, and if it comes up heads you have to buy the drinks. Seems fair enough — a fifty-fifty chance — so you agree.

He tosses the coin. It comes up heads. You buy the drinks. You grit your teeth and head off to the bar.

Come the next round, Pete makes the same offer. You agree. Heads again. Then again. And again.

After five consecutive heads, the probability of that sequence — assuming a perfectly fair coin — is 0.5x 0.5 x 0.5 x 0.5 x 0.5 or (½)⁵ = 0.031. This falls below the conventional significance threshold of α = .05 (or p=<0.05). In other words, five heads in a row is, by the standard logic of null hypothesis significance testing, a statistically significant result: we would reject the null hypothesis that Pete's coin is fair.

Why p < .05? The 5% threshold was popularised by Ronald Fisher in the 1920s — a convenient rule of thumb rather than a mathematical law. Its ubiquity in science is largely historical accident. The coin-toss example gives you an intuitive feel for where that number sits: five consecutive heads is just "suspicious enough."

A note on the gambler's fallacy

Notice that after four heads you might have felt sure that "tails must be due now." This is the gambler's fallacy: the mistaken belief that past independent outcomes influence future ones. For a fair coin, each toss is entirely independent. Pete's fifth toss has exactly the same probability of landing tails as his very first one did.

What does accumulate is the joint probability of the full sequence — the p-value. We are not saying tails is more likely next time; we are saying that the overall pattern of five heads is increasingly hard to explain by chance.

Part 2

What You Already Know About Pete

Here is the crucial insight: the decision to "reject the null hypothesis" does not happen in a vacuum. It depends — whether we admit it or not — on what we already believe before the data arrive. In Bayesian statistics this is called the prior probability.

The same five heads can warrant very different conclusions depending on who Pete is. Select a version of Pete below:

Pete is scrupulously honest
You have known him for years. He would be mortified at the thought of cheating anyone out of anything. He once handed back £5 change that a cashier gave him by mistake.
Pete is a bit of a joker
Pete loves a prank. He once put googly eyes on every item in the office fridge. Coin tricks are exactly the kind of thing he would attempt.
Pete is a notorious cheat
Pete was banned from the local poker night last year. Everyone has heard the stories. You are fairly sure he owns trick coins.
Even with five heads — a statistically significant result — your strong prior that Pete is honest gives you good reason to remain sceptical that the coin is rigged. Five heads is improbable, but so is honest Pete deciding to cheat. The p-value tells you the probability of the data given the null hypothesis; it says nothing about the probability of the null hypothesis given your prior beliefs. These are very different quantities.

This is exactly what Bayesian inference formalises. Rather than asking only "how likely are these data if nothing is going on?", it combines the evidence with our prior to produce an updated posterior belief:

Posterior ∝ Likelihood × Prior

Different priors, same data, legitimately different conclusions. This is not a flaw or subjectivity run amok — it is a mathematically coherent account of how rational beliefs should update.

Part 3

The Octopus

Case study — 2010 FIFA World Cup — Wikipedia

Paul the Octopus was housed at the Sea Life Centre in Oberhausen, Germany. Before each match, keepers placed food in two boxes labelled with the flags of the competing teams. Paul correctly chose the winning team in all seven of Germany's 2010 World Cup matches, and also correctly predicted Spain as the winner of the final — eight correct predictions in a row. The probability of achieving this by chance is (½)⁸ = 0.0039 — far below the p < .05 threshold.

By exactly the same frequentist logic we applied to Pete, we should decisively reject the null hypothesis that Paul was guessing at random. Does that mean we should conclude that Paul the Octopus was genuinely psychic?

Almost certainly not — and this is not a failure of logic. It is a demonstration of how our prior beliefs modulate what any given p-value actually means. Most people's prior probability that an octopus possesses genuine precognitive ability is extraordinarily small — close to zero. When the prior is that extreme, even a p-value of 0.016 barely shifts the posterior. The evidence needed to seriously move our belief would have to be vastly more compelling than six lucky choices.

Carl Sagan captured this intuition: "Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence." Bayesian reasoning makes this precise: the lower your prior probability for a claim, the more decisive the evidence must be to raise the posterior to a credible level.

The octopus example is particularly instructive because it shows that a "statistically significant" result is not the same as a scientifically meaningful or even plausible one. Statistical significance is a statement about data rarity under a null model — not a certificate of truth.

Summary

Three Things to Take Away

1
A p-value is a conditional probability. It tells you how likely your data are if the null hypothesis is true — not how likely the null hypothesis is given your data. p(data | H₀) ≠ p(H₀ | data). Confusing these two is sometimes called the "inverse probability fallacy."
2
The conventional threshold (α = .05) is arbitrary. It has historical roots and a certain intuitive feel, but it is not a law of nature. Crossing it does not make a result "true," and falling short of it does not make a result "false." Statistical significance is not the same as practical or theoretical significance.
3
Prior knowledge always plays a role. Whether you are suspicious of Pete after three heads or five depends on what you already know about him. Making those prior beliefs explicit — as Bayesian methods require — is not a weakness; it is intellectual honesty about the reasoning we were doing implicitly all along - how we interpret sequences of observed events often depends on the context and our expectations.
Discussion prompt: Can you think of a real scientific finding you have come across where the prior probability of the claim should have made people much more sceptical of a significant p-value? What would it take to actually convince you?
